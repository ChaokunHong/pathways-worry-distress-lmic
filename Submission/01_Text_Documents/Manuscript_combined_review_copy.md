# Introduction

When a large-scale public health crisis strikes, mental health researchers typically ask how much distress a population experiences, documenting the proportion screening positive for depression, anxiety, or stress on standardised instruments [1-3]. Less commonly asked is whether the diverse concerns generated by a crisis (fears of infection, lost income, food shortages, social exclusion, stigma) are associated with these distress dimensions in the same way, or whether different types of concern map onto different symptom profiles. This question matters because, where mental health systems are already under-resourced [4,5], knowing which domains of worry are most strongly associated with which dimensions of distress could inform more specific and efficient mental health responses.

Beyond well-documented sociodemographic risk factors (age, gender, education, employment, comorbidity; reviewed in [6,7]), the crisis-related concerns themselves merit closer analytical attention. Where the Depression Anxiety Stress Scales (DASS-21) [8] are used, the three subscales are typically modelled as separate outcomes with a shared predictor set. The tripartite model on which the DASS was built holds that depression and anxiety share a common component of general negative affect but are differentiated by low positive affect (specific to depression) and physiological hyperarousal (specific to anxiety), while the stress subscale captures a broader state of tension and irritability [9]. If these symptom dimensions are partially distinct in their affective structure, their associations with external stressors may also differ. Yet this possibility is rarely examined directly. Studies using the DASS-21 during the pandemic can reveal that a given predictor reaches significance for one subscale but not another: Lenzo et al. [10] found different emotional regulation predictors for each DASS-21 dimension in Italian healthcare workers; Pandey et al. [11] reported that occupational role predicted anxiety but not depression or stress among Nepalese healthcare workers; and Poudel et al. [12] found that predictors differed across the three DASS-21 subscales in a Nepalese general-population sample, with disturbed family relations predicting anxiety and stress but not depression, while COVID-19 infection predicted depression and anxiety but not stress. In each case, the cross-subscale differences were noted incidentally rather than treated as a substantive finding requiring theoretical interpretation. Meta-analyses have similarly reported pooled prevalence and predictor effects for depression, anxiety, and stress in parallel without assessing whether different categories of stressor carry systematically different associations with each symptom dimension [2,13].

On the predictor side, a parallel issue arises with how crisis-related worry is measured. Unidimensional instruments such as the Fear of COVID-19 Scale [14] yield a single score. Where surveys include multi-item worry inventories covering heterogeneous concerns, these items are commonly summed into a single composite. This analytical convention treats different types of concern as interchangeable in their association with distress. Taylor et al.'s [15] COVID Stress Scales are a notable counterexample, identifying five empirically distinguishable facets of pandemic-related distress. However, Taylor et al.'s facets describe varieties of stress *response* (danger fears, contamination anxiety, xenophobia, traumatic stress, compulsive checking); our interest is in varieties of stressor *exposure*, the material, social, and symbolic concerns that characterise the crisis experience, particularly in resource-constrained settings where worries about food security, livelihood, and stigma may be more salient than contamination anxiety or compulsive checking. Moreover, even multidimensional stress instruments have primarily been used to examine the internal structure of pandemic stress rather than to ask whether different stress or worry domains carry different associations with different distress outcomes. This joint question, whether decomposing worry reveals differential associations across symptom dimensions, has seldom been centred as an empirical question in its own right.

We draw on the Stress Process Model [16,17] to motivate this decomposition. The model's concept of *stressor proliferation*, the tendency for a primary stressor to cascade into secondary concerns across economic, social, and personal domains, describes the crisis experience well: an initial health threat rapidly generates livelihood loss, family disruption, food insecurity, and fears of social exclusion [18]. Because this proliferation is shaped by social position, an aggregate worry score may collapse variation that matters for distinguishing which symptom dimensions are most strongly implicated. We complement this with Hobfoll's [19,20] Conservation of Resources theory, which suggests that threats to different types of resource (material, social, symbolic) may be associated with different forms of distress: worrying about whether one can feed one's family may not carry the same psychological signature as worrying about being stigmatised by neighbours. We treat this as an orienting framework rather than a set of precise predictions.

We test this using data from the SPEAR study (Social, Psychological, and Economic Impact of COVID-19 Among Residents), a cross-sectional household survey conducted across 13 community sites in Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam during February to June 2021. The survey originally scored its 21-item worry inventory as a single composite; the present study decomposes it into five validated sub-domains. The central aim is to test whether these domains (health fears, economic worry, basic-needs worry, social disruption, and safety/stigma) show differential associations with depression, anxiety, and stress as measured by the DASS-21.

In support of this central question, we also (a) validate the five-domain structure using split-sample factor analysis; (b) examine whether the joint pattern of associations among livelihood disruption, perceived health risk, specific worry domains, and specific symptom dimensions is consistent with hypothesised relationships among these variables (all measured concurrently; no directional inference is drawn); and (c) describe how worry-distress association patterns vary across the three country contexts. These countries differ in economic structure, healthcare capacity, pandemic timing, and cultural norms. Metric measurement invariance across the three countries was tested and supported for both the DASS-21 and the five-factor worry model (Methods §2.3), licensing comparison of loading-weighted coefficients across countries; scalar invariance was not supported, so latent means are not compared and country-level analyses are treated as descriptive. The limited number of community clusters (k = 13) and purposive element in site selection further constrain cross-national inference.

If differential worry-distress associations are observed, this would carry both empirical and conceptual implications: empirically, it would demonstrate that the predominant aggregate-score approach obscures meaningful variation; conceptually, it would suggest that psychological distress during a public health crisis is not a uniform response to generalised threat but a set of differentiated responses to specific domains of concern, a pattern that, if replicated in longitudinal and intervention research, could inform more targeted mental health programming.

# Methods

## 2.1 Study Design and Population

We conducted a secondary analysis of cross-sectional household survey data from the SPEAR study [21], collected between February and June 2021 at 13 community sites across Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam (Figure 1; urban, peri-urban, rural, and remote settings as defined in [21]). The data-collection window encompassed distinct national pandemic phases. Households were recruited by purposive site selection with random within-site sampling; one adult per household (aged ≥ 18, consenting, competent in the local interview language) completed a structured interview. Fieldwork procedures and translation are reported in [21].

Two groups were excluded from the primary analysis: respondents with missing site identifier, and Vietnamese respondents recruited outside the four pre-specified sites during a protocol extension. The primary analytic sample comprised n = 1,462 across 13 protocol-specified sites. The parent SPEAR study [21] was reviewed and approved by the National Hospital for Tropical Diseases Ethics Committee (Hanoi, Vietnam), the Hospital for Tropical Diseases Ethics Committee (Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam), the Ethics Committee of the Nepal Health Research Council (Kathmandu, Nepal), the Patan Hospital Ethics Committee (Kathmandu, Nepal), the Ethics Committee of the Faculty of Medicine, University of Indonesia (Jakarta, Indonesia), and the Oxford Tropical Research Ethics Committee (Oxford, UK); local government permission was obtained in each context as required by local regulations. Reporting follows the STROBE recommendations [22].

## 2.2 Measures

*Psychological distress.* The primary outcomes were the depression, anxiety, and stress subscales of the DASS-21 [8] (7 items each, 4-point Likert 0–3), prorated when ≥ 6 of 7 items were complete, multiplied by two for DASS-42 equivalence, and z-standardised for regression analyses. The DASS-21 has been validated in Southeast Asian LMIC settings [23,24]; translation and pilot procedures are reported in [21]. Psychometric properties are in Table 2.

*Crisis-related worry.* The primary exposures were five sub-domains of crisis-related worry, constructed from a bespoke 21-item inventory developed for the SPEAR study [21]. Each item was rated on a 4-point Likert scale (0 = *not at all worried* to 3 = *extremely worried*). The five sub-domains were: health fears (5 items; range 0–15); economic worry (7 items; 0–21); basic-needs worry (3 items; 0–9); social disruption (4 items; 0–12); and safety/stigma (2 items; 0–6). Item-level composition, scoring rules, and reliability are in Table S1. The five-factor structure was validated by split-sample factor analysis and its cross-country measurement invariance assessed alongside the DASS-21 (see below).

*Covariates.* Covariates were organised into two blocks informed by the Stress Process Model [16] and Conservation of Resources theory [19]. Block 1 (demographics) included age, gender, ethnic-minority status, educational attainment, employment status, self-rated general health, and comorbidity count. Block 2 (crisis context) comprised perceived health threat, self-assessed infection risk, health-system trust, and household livelihood disruption (Table S1). Exploratory moderators included a binary indicator for any healthcare worker in the household and a site-level community-support scale (Table S1). Variable coding rules are documented in Table S1.

## 2.3 Statistical Analysis

Figure 2 provides a visual overview of the analytical pipeline. All analyses were conducted in R version 4.4.3; package versions and analysis scripts are available at https://github.com/ChaokunHong/pathways-worry-distress-lmic (archived at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19631187).

*Measurement validation and missing data.* The worry inventory's five-factor structure was validated by split-sample exploratory (n = 660; maximum-likelihood extraction, oblimin rotation, polychoric correlations [25]; Horn's parallel analysis [26]) and confirmatory factor analysis (hold-out n = 660; WLSMV estimator [27,28]; Table S2). Multi-group CFA was conducted to assess measurement invariance across the three countries for both instruments (Tables S3, S4; Chen [29]; Putnick & Bornstein [30]). Covariate missingness affected a substantial proportion of the sample (Figure 1) and was handled by MICE under a missing-at-random assumption (m = 30 imputations, 10 iterations, seed = 2026 [31,32]); diagnostics are in Tables S5–S6 and Figure S1. Coefficients were pooled via Rubin's rules [33,37].

*Primary multilevel models.* Each z-standardised DASS-21 subscale was modelled as a function of the five worry sub-domains, adjusting for Block 1, Block 2, and country. Linear mixed-effects models with a random intercept for site were fitted on each imputed dataset and pooled via Rubin's rules [34–36]. Each worry coefficient represents the associated difference in z-scored outcome per one-unit increase in the raw worry sub-domain score. Because k = 13 clusters falls below Maas–Hox thresholds [38], only fixed effects are interpreted and cross-level interactions are flagged as preliminary (Supplementary Note S1). To quantify incremental variance, we fitted nested models from M0 (country + site) through M1 (+ Block 1) and M2 (+ Block 2) to the primary M3 (+ five worry sub-domains); the block ordering is for variance-partitioning purposes and does not imply causal sequence. Marginal R² [40] was averaged across imputations [41]; Cohen's f² is in Table S8. Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction [42] was applied across the 15 pre-specified worry × outcome tests. Multicollinearity and residual diagnostics are in Table S7 and Figure S2.

*Supplementary, exploratory, and sensitivity analyses.* M3 was re-estimated separately by country (complete-case n = 892; within-country k = 3/6/4). Five pre-specified mediation pathways linking distal exposures to DASS-21 outcomes through worry sub-domains were examined using the Schomaker–Heumann [44] bootstrap-after-imputation framework (Rubin-pooled; Table S9); cross-sectional mediation is a decomposition of covariation, not a causal estimate [43]. An exploratory SEM encountered identification issues in both specifications (Table S10; Supplementary Note S2). Four exploratory moderation families were tested: community-support × worry (15 tests); stigma contextual decomposition (within- vs between-site [45]) and stigma × HCW interaction; worry × location type (45 tests); and gender × worry and age × worry (30 tests; Table S11). Five pre-specified sensitivity analyses were conducted: complete-case re-estimation, aggregate worry as single predictor, multilevel logistic regression for binary DASS caseness (Table S12), CR2 cluster-robust SEs [39], and exclusion of two potentially criterion-contaminated items. Post-hoc diagnostics (MI congeniality, attenuation correction, leave-one-site-out, Harman's CMV test, age spline, 14-site inclusion) are in Tables S6, S13–S17. No a priori power calculation was performed; post-hoc minimum-detectable-effect analysis is in the Table 4 notes. The analysis scripts, imputation specification, and variable codebook are available at https://github.com/ChaokunHong/pathways-worry-distress-lmic (archived at https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19631187).

## Supplementary Note S1 — Cluster-count justification for k = 13

Multilevel linear modelling with k = 13 clusters falls below the Maas and Hox
(2005; [38]) benchmark of k ≈ 30 for reasonably calibrated fixed-effect
standard errors and their k ≥ 50 benchmark for variance-component
interpretation. We treat this as a substantive constraint rather than a
contraindication, for three reasons.

First, REML fixed-effect *point estimates* remain approximately unbiased at
small k; the Maas–Hox concern is standard-error calibration, not bias. We
therefore report fixed-effect coefficients with the caveat that their
nominal confidence intervals are likely slightly too narrow in the direction
of anti-conservatism.

Second, to bound this anti-conservative direction we fit CR2 cluster-robust
standard errors with Satterthwaite adjustment (Pustejovsky and Tipton 2018;
[39]) as a pre-specified sensitivity check. In preliminary comparisons,
CR2 Satterthwaite degrees of freedom contracted to 2–5 for several of the
worry coefficients. At such low df the CR2-based confidence intervals are
several-fold wider than the REML-based intervals and are treated as a
conservative upper bound on inference rather than as the primary inferential
framework.

Third, we deliberately do *not* interpret individual site-level variance
components or individual site random-intercept BLUPs, both because of the
Maas–Hox k ≥ 50 benchmark for variance-component precision and because the
purposive site-selection strategy of the parent SPEAR study (§2.1) makes the
13 sites non-exchangeable in a way that conventional random-effect theory
assumes. Between-site clustering is used solely to adjust the fixed-effect
standard errors for within-site correlation. Cross-level interactions
involving the site-level community-support scale are explicitly flagged as
preliminary.

## Supplementary Note S2 — Structural equation model specifications

Two confirmatory SEMs were fitted in *lavaan* to jointly model the worry
latent structure and its associations with the DASS-21 factors.

**Five-factor specification** (theorised structure): worry indicators load
on five correlated factors (health fears, economic, basic needs, social,
safety/stigma) identical to the primary-regression decomposition in §2.2,
with the three DASS-21 factors (depression, anxiety, stress) regressed on
all five worry factors plus perceived risk, comorbidity, age, and gender.
Fitted with WLSMV on ordinal indicators: CFI = 0.997, TLI = 0.998,
RMSEA = 0.054, SRMR = 0.080 (Table S10). Although the global fit indices
exceed conventional thresholds, the post-estimation check flagged negative
residual variance for at least one indicator, producing a warning
consistent with empirical under-identification at this level of
worry-sub-domain disaggregation.

**Four-factor material-deprivation specification**: basic-needs and economic
worry are merged into a single material-deprivation factor; the remaining
three worry factors and the DASS-21 structure are unchanged. Fitted with
WLSMV: CFI = 0.997, TLI = 0.997, RMSEA = 0.058. The post-estimation check
still flags negative residual variance, indicating that the identification
concern is *not* resolved by factor merging. Both specifications reproduce
the directional pattern of worry → DASS-21 associations found in the
multilevel regression; neither is used for primary inference.

A pure-CFA refit of the five-factor worry measurement model under MLR
(no structural paths, no DASS-21 indicators; Table S10 Panel C) yielded
CFI = 0.837 and RMSEA = 0.070 — the appropriate reference for absolute
fit under MLR with ordinal items. Methodological and substantive
interpretation of both SEMs is bounded by these identification warnings;
the multilevel regression remains the primary analytic framework, and SEM
results are reported as a convergence check rather than as an independent
inferential model.

# Results

## 3.1 Sample

Of 1,462 adults retained for primary analysis, 607 (41.5%) were enrolled in Indonesia, 460 (31.5%) in Nepal, and 395 (27.0%) in Vietnam (Table 1). The analytic sample was middle-aged (overall mean 38 years, SD 12) and predominantly female (59.3%), with younger and more female-skewed composition in Vietnam (mean 35 years; 70% female) than in Indonesia or Nepal (both mean 39 years; 62% and 48% female, respectively). Self-identified ethnic-minority membership was common in Indonesia (77%) and Nepal (76%) but uncommon in Vietnam (23%), reflecting the purposive sampling of minority-concentrated sites in the two former settings. Country distributions differed across age, gender, ethnicity, education, employment, self-rated health, comorbidity count, perceived health threat, self-assessed infection risk, and health-system trust (Table 1; Kruskal–Wallis and χ² *p* < 0.001 for each), whereas household livelihood disruption did not differ across countries (Kruskal–Wallis *p* = 0.43). Unadjusted DASS-21 mean scores (DASS-42-equivalent metric after ×2 prorating; §2.2) were 3.7 (SD 5.6) for depression, 3.6 (5.0) for anxiety, and 5.4 (6.3) for stress; Nepalese respondents scored slightly higher and Vietnamese respondents slightly lower on all three subscales. Covariate missingness was non-trivial (Table S5) and was addressed by multiple imputation (*m* = 30); imputation diagnostics and congeniality checks are in Table S6 and Figure S1.

## 3.2 Psychometric properties and measurement invariance

*Reliability.* Internal consistency was acceptable to good for all primary scales (Table 2). For the DASS-21 subscales, Cronbach's α was 0.87 for depression, 0.81 for anxiety, and 0.86 for stress, with McDonald's ω total of 0.87, 0.82, and 0.87. Among the five worry sub-domains, α ranged from 0.68 (social disruption; 4 items) to 0.87 (health fears); ω total ranged from 0.69 to 0.88. The full 21-item worry inventory showed α = 0.92.

*Factor structure and invariance.* The five-factor worry solution was supported by split-sample exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis (hold-out CFA: CFI = 0.990, TLI = 0.988, RMSEA = 0.068, SRMR = 0.076; Table S2). Multi-group CFA across Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam supported metric invariance for both the DASS-21 (ΔCFI = −0.007 vs. configural) and the worry inventory (ΔCFI = −0.005; Tables S3–S4). Full scalar invariance was not supported (ΔCFI = −0.031 for both instruments relative to metric), but **partial scalar invariance with four of 21 intercepts freed** was supported for both (ΔCFI = −0.008 for the DASS-21 and −0.010 for the worry inventory; Tables S3–S4). This permits comparison of slopes and their ordering across countries while precluding direct comparison of latent means. Country-level comparisons of *levels* are therefore treated as descriptive only; within-country slope estimates remain interpretable.

## 3.3 Descriptive associations

The five worry sub-domains were moderately to strongly inter-correlated (Spearman *r* = 0.29–0.70; Table 3), with the strongest pairs being basic-needs × safety/stigma (*r* = 0.70), economic × basic-needs (*r* = 0.69), and health fears × safety/stigma (*r* = 0.61). Each worry domain correlated with each DASS-21 outcome in the expected direction (*r* = 0.27–0.37). At the bivariate level, basic-needs worry had the strongest correlation with depression (*r* = 0.37), health fears with anxiety (*r* = 0.37), and both health fears and economic worry with stress (*r* ≈ 0.36). Country-level distributions of worry and DASS scores are shown as violin plots in Figure 3; Nepalese respondents reported higher mean scores on health fears, economic worry, basic-needs worry, and safety/stigma than those in Indonesia or Vietnam. The five worry sub-domains differed in the extent of between-site clustering: basic-needs worry and safety/stigma were the most site-clustered (ICC = 0.43 and 0.36, respectively), whereas economic and social worries were more evenly distributed across sites (ICC = 0.13 and 0.14; Table S7). Variance-inflation factors across the full M3 covariate set remained below 3.6 (highest: basic-needs worry, VIF = 3.56; Table S7), with no pair exceeding conventional multicollinearity thresholds for linear regression.

## 3.4 Differential associations between worry sub-domains and distress

*Variance explained.* Marginal R² rose from 0.005–0.010 in the intercept-only M0 to 0.234, 0.258, and 0.252 in the primary M3 for depression, anxiety, and stress, respectively (Figure 5). The five worry sub-domains were the largest single contributor to explained variance in all three outcomes (ΔR² = 0.124 for depression, 0.113 for anxiety, 0.129 for stress), exceeding the joint contribution of the seven demographic covariates in Block 1 (ΔR² = 0.068–0.076) and the four contextual covariates in Block 2 (ΔR² = 0.035–0.058).

*Differential worry signatures.* Pooled across imputations and adjusted for both covariate blocks and country (Indonesia as reference), the five worry sub-domains displayed clearly differential associations with the three DASS-21 outcomes (Figure 4A, Table 4; Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted *p* across the 15 pre-specified worry × outcome tests). Health fears were associated with anxiety (β = 0.055, 95% CI 0.030–0.080, *p*_adj < 0.001) and stress (β = 0.030, 0.006–0.054, *p*_adj = 0.024) but not with depression (β = −0.011, −0.035–0.014, *p*_adj = 0.42). Economic worry was associated with depression (β = 0.028, 0.008–0.049, *p*_adj = 0.011) and stress (β = 0.033, 0.013–0.053, *p*_adj = 0.003) but not with anxiety (*p*_adj = 0.26). Basic-needs worry was associated with depression (β = 0.074, 0.035–0.113, *p*_adj < 0.001) and stress (β = 0.047, 0.008–0.086, *p*_adj = 0.026) but did not reach significance for anxiety after adjustment (β = 0.037, *p*_adj = 0.085). Social disruption was associated with all three outcomes (depression β = 0.051, 0.028–0.074, *p*_adj < 0.001; anxiety β = 0.043, 0.019–0.066, *p*_adj = 0.001; stress β = 0.068, 0.045–0.091, *p*_adj < 0.001). Safety/stigma was associated with depression (β = 0.091, 0.035–0.146, *p*_adj = 0.003) and anxiety (β = 0.075, 0.020–0.130, *p*_adj = 0.013) but not with stress (β = 0.021, *p*_adj = 0.44).

Taken together, the pattern of associations is consistent with the differentiated-response hypothesis motivating §1, and we describe it here as **selective pathway specificity**: each symptom dimension was associated with a distinct subset of worry sub-domains rather than with worry as a whole. Eleven of the 15 pre-specified worry × outcome tests survived BH-FDR adjustment. Four of the five worry sub-domains showed a selective pattern — each associated with only two of the three outcomes: health fears were associated with threat-related symptoms (anxiety, stress); material worries (economic, basic needs) with mood-related symptoms (depression, stress); and safety/stigma with mood and anxiety symptoms. Social disruption was the single exception, showing associations with all three outcomes. The pairwise ordering of β across sub-domains was not formally tested for heterogeneity; specificity should be read as a qualitative pattern observed across the pre-specified tests after BH-FDR, rather than as a tested effect-size contrast.

*Covariate findings.* Across all three outcomes, age (β ≈ −0.010 to −0.012 per year), higher comorbidity count (β ≈ 0.23–0.25 per condition), poorer self-rated general health (β ≈ −0.13 to −0.18), and higher perceived health threat (β ≈ 0.012 to 0.019) were independently associated with greater distress (all *p* < 0.01; see Figure 4B for point estimates and 95% CIs). Female respondents scored higher on stress only (β = 0.15, *p* = 0.003); students (*n* = 53; 3.9% of the analytic sample) scored higher on depression than full-time workers (β = 0.39, *p* = 0.002); lower health-system trust was associated with greater stress (β = −0.036, *p* = 0.014); and self-assessed infection risk was associated with stress only (β = 0.08, *p* = 0.03). Covariate *p* values are raw (uncorrected), as covariate coefficients lie outside the pre-specified 15-test worry × outcome family; selective single-outcome associations (female, student, health trust, self-assessed risk) should therefore be read as adjustment quantities rather than as confirmatory findings. Country main effects were modest after adjustment: relative to Indonesia, Nepalese and Vietnamese respondents had lower adjusted anxiety (*p* ≤ 0.004), with no adjusted country differences for depression or stress. Household livelihood disruption showed no residual direct association with any DASS outcome once worry sub-domains were in the model, pointing to §3.5 for the decomposition of this covariation through worry.

## 3.5 Mediation: decomposition of covariation

All five pre-specified mediation pathways produced Rubin-pooled indirect effects with 95% confidence intervals excluding zero (Table S9; Figure 6). Household livelihood disruption covaried with depression through economic worry (indirect β = 0.033, 95% CI 0.022–0.045; 67% of the total association transmitted through this single pathway) and through basic-needs worry (β = 0.025, 0.016–0.035; 52%). Perceived health threat covaried with anxiety and stress through health fears (anxiety: β = 0.015, 0.010–0.021, 44%; stress: β = 0.015, 0.010–0.020, 49%) and with depression through safety/stigma (β = 0.009, 0.005–0.013, 37%). In the joint M3 model containing all five worry sub-domains, livelihood disruption no longer carried a significant direct coefficient on any DASS outcome (§3.4; *p* > 0.08 in all three outcomes), consistent with covariation being accommodated by the worry block as a whole; the single-mediator Schomaker–Heumann decompositions here nonetheless show a nominally significant ADE for the livelihood → basic-needs → depression path (ADE = 0.024, 95% CI 0.004–0.043; Table S9), indicating that the absorption interpretation is specific to the joint-model specification rather than an invariant property. Perceived health threat, by contrast, retained direct associations with all three outcomes in M3 (§3.4), with the mediation pathways through health fears (anxiety, stress) and safety/stigma (depression) accounting for 37–49% of the total association. Taken together, this pattern is consistent with the Stress Process Model expectation that distal structural exposures reach psychological outcomes in part through the specific worries they proliferate into, with the relative degree of proliferation varying by exposure type. Complete-case bootstrap results (Table 5) were directionally consistent with the Rubin-pooled estimates, albeit with wider intervals. Given the cross-sectional design, these coefficients should be interpreted as a decomposition of observed covariation under a hypothesised direction, not as causal estimates [43]. A structural-equation specification of the same measurement–structural model yielded improper solutions in both configurations (negative residual variance for at least one indicator; Supplementary Note S2, Table S10); the global fit indices produced under these configurations (CFI ≥ 0.997) therefore cannot be interpreted in the usual way. The coefficient signs were nonetheless consistent in direction with the regression-based estimates, and the SEM is reported as a directional convergence check only, not as an independent inferential test.

## 3.6 Country-stratified estimates

Country-stratified multilevel estimates (Table 6, Figure 7) are presented descriptively because each within-country cluster count (Indonesia *k* = 3, Nepal *k* = 6, Vietnam *k* = 4) is below the Maas–Hox threshold for reliable variance-component estimation (Note S1), and because the non-support for full scalar invariance (§3.2) precludes inferential level comparisons; asterisks in Figure 7 denote fixed-effect *p* < 0.05, between-site variance components are not interpreted, and no multiple-comparison correction is applied across the 45 country × worry × outcome cells. Within these descriptive caveats, the pooled signature of §3.4 was not uniform across sites. In Indonesia, the largest within-country coefficients were for safety/stigma across all three outcomes (β = 0.320–0.558), whereas economic and health-fear coefficients were near zero. In Nepal, economic worry carried the most consistent coefficients across outcomes (β = 0.040–0.074), and safety/stigma remained associated with depression. In Vietnam, social disruption carried the largest coefficients across outcomes (β = 0.055–0.104, significant for depression and stress but not anxiety), while health fears carried a sign opposite to the pooled estimate in both Nepal (for depression) and Vietnam (for all three outcomes). These country-specific deviations should be treated as hypothesis-generating given the small within-country *k*, the descriptive status of scalar-level comparisons, and the potential for multicollinearity-driven sign shifts among correlated worry sub-domains.

## 3.7 Sensitivity and exploratory analyses

Five pre-specified sensitivity analyses gave results directionally consistent with the primary model: complete-case re-estimation (*n* = 892), aggregate-worry single predictor, multilevel logistic regression for binary DASS caseness (Table S12), CR2 cluster-robust standard errors, and exclusion of two potentially criterion-contaminated items. Post-hoc diagnostics further confirmed the stability of the headline pattern: MI congeniality comparison (Table S6), attenuation-corrected estimates (Table S13), leave-one-site-out forest (Table S14, Figure S5), Harman's single-factor test (Table S15; first factor variance < 50%), age-spline re-estimation (Table S16), and the 14-site inclusion (Table S17) each reproduced the primary ordering of worry × outcome associations. Among exploratory moderation families (Table S11), community-support × worry and worry × location-type simple slopes (Figures S3–S4) produced scattered single-panel effects that did not survive family-wise adjustment; the stigma × healthcare-worker-in-household and gender × worry / age × worry families likewise produced no robust interactions. These exploratory tests are reported for transparency and do not modify the primary findings.

# Discussion

## 4.1 Principal findings

Decomposing a previously unidimensional 21-item worry inventory into five sub-domains revealed distinctly differential associations with depression, anxiety, and stress. Eleven of the 15 pre-specified worry × outcome associations survived Benjamini–Hochberg adjustment, and four of the five sub-domains were associated with only two of the three distress dimensions, producing a descriptive pattern across the pre-specified tests that we term **selective pathway specificity** (not itself a tested effect-size contrast): material worries (economic, basic-needs) were associated with mood-related symptoms (depression, stress); health fears with threat-related symptoms (anxiety, stress); safety/stigma with mood and anxiety; and only social disruption was associated with all three. Two features extend this headline finding. First, the five-worry block was the single largest contributor to explained variance in each outcome, with ΔR² = 0.113–0.129 — larger than the seven demographic covariates in Block 1 (ΔR² = 0.068–0.076) and larger than the four contextual covariates in Block 2 (ΔR² = 0.035–0.058), and comparable in magnitude to the two covariate blocks combined. This indicates that the domain-level content of worry — and not only well-documented structural risk markers — carries substantive explanatory weight. Second, the two worry sub-domains with the highest between-site clustering (basic-needs worry, ICC = 0.43; safety/stigma, ICC = 0.36) were also the two largest predictors of depression, indicating that the depressive signal is most tightly coupled with stressors whose distribution is itself most patterned by community context. These findings imply that aggregate approaches to crisis-related worry both under-specify the psychological signature of distress and under-represent the structural layer that patterns where its burden concentrates.

## 4.2 Aggregate worry scores obscure a structured pattern

The Introduction noted that DASS-21 studies have repeatedly reported cross-subscale heterogeneity in predictor effects without treating the heterogeneity itself as a substantive finding [10–12], and that multi-item worry inventories have conventionally been aggregated into a single composite [15]. The present data allow the empirical question behind these observations to be answered directly. In our aggregate-worry sensitivity analysis (§3.7), a single summed worry score reproduced the overall direction of association with all three DASS-21 outcomes but collapsed the differential signature: the information that basic-needs worry is specifically associated with depression but not with anxiety after adjustment, and that health fears show the opposite pattern, is only available after decomposition. This empirical contrast — same sample, same outcomes, same covariates, structurally different conclusions depending on whether the exposure is aggregated — is, to our reading, the strongest evidence in the study that aggregate scoring discards information that is both theoretically and practically meaningful. Read together with the cross-subscale heterogeneity repeatedly reported in the wider literature [2,13,59,60], our findings suggest that this heterogeneity is neither incidental nor artefactual: once the exposure side is itself decomposed, a structured mapping between types of stressor and dimensions of distress becomes visible. At a moment when global mental health research is moving towards demand-side assessment and more precisely targeted intervention, this has methodological implications for how secondary analyses of large crisis-period datasets are designed and what they are capable of detecting.

## 4.3 Stressor proliferation is not symptom-uniform

These findings extend the Stress Process Model [16–18] in a specific direction. Pearlin and colleagues' concept of stressor proliferation — that an initial stressor cascades into secondary concerns across economic, social, and symbolic domains — predicts that a public-health shock would generate a broad spectrum of worries. Our data are consistent with this expectation: the five worry sub-domains were moderately to strongly inter-correlated (*r* = 0.29–0.70), and two distal exposures (perceived health threat, household livelihood disruption) covaried with distress partly through these specific worry channels (§3.5). What the proliferation account alone does not predict is that the proliferated stressors would map onto distress dimensions in a structured, non-uniform way. The selective specificity we observe suggests a refinement: proliferation is not symptom-uniform in its psychological signature. Different components of the proliferated bundle — material, interpersonal, symbolic — appear to resonate with different aspects of negative affect, broadly consistent with the tripartite model's separation of low positive affect (specific to depression), physiological hyperarousal (specific to anxiety), and generalised tension [9].

Conservation of Resources theory [19,20] offers a complementary interpretive frame. If threats to different resource classes generate qualitatively distinct psychological signatures, one would expect basic-needs and economic worries — both direct threats to material resources — to track depressive symptoms, which involve loss of positive affect and motivation; one would expect health fears, anchored in anticipated physical threat, to track anxiety symptoms, which involve hyperarousal; and one would expect safety/stigma concerns, which threaten moral standing and social status [46,47], to track both mood and anxiety symptoms but not the more reality-anchored tension captured by the stress subscale. Each of these expectations is broadly consistent with the observed pattern, though the ordering of effect sizes across sub-domains was not formally tested and should not be read as a confirmed effect-size contrast.

Our findings sit alongside a substantial empirical literature on specific pathways that has developed in parallel but rarely been integrated. The association we observe between material worries and depression is consistent with Jones's 149-country analysis linking food insecurity to depression in a dose-response fashion [49], with Guan and colleagues' systematic review of 40 studies identifying financial stress as one of the most reproducible correlates of depressive symptoms [58], and with Ridley and colleagues' review of natural-experiment evidence that negative income shocks worsen depression and anxiety [48]. The association between stigma worries and both depression and anxiety is consistent with Link and Phelan's formulation of stigma as a process producing status loss and social exclusion [46], and with Yang and colleagues' extension that locates its psychological impact in the threat to what locally matters most [47]. These converging pathway-specific literatures, however, have largely been read in isolation from one another. The value-added of the present decomposition is that it places these separate pathway accounts within a single analytic frame — one in which the differential associations are no longer surprising, but expected.

## 4.4 The structural patterning of distress: social determinants and community context

Beyond the individual-level specificity pattern, our data point to a structural feature of psychological distress in LMIC community settings that is of particular interest for social science audiences. The two worry sub-domains most strongly patterned by local site — basic-needs worry (ICC = 0.43) and safety/stigma (ICC = 0.36) — were also the two largest predictors of depression in the pooled model (β = 0.074 and 0.091). The correspondence across the full set of five sub-domains is positive but non-monotonic (Spearman ρ between ICC and β_depression = 0.6, not formally tested at *n* = 5): health fears show mid-range site clustering (ICC = 0.23) but the smallest depression coefficient (β = −0.011, n.s.), while the two least-clustered domains (economic worry ICC = 0.13, β = 0.028; social disruption ICC = 0.14, β = 0.051) carry smaller coefficients than the two most-clustered domains. The pattern is therefore most defensible at the extremes of site clustering rather than as a strict monotonic rank correspondence; it does not establish that community context drives the depression association, since individual and community processes cannot be cleanly separated with the 13 clusters available here and within-site variation also contributes to each coefficient. What the correspondence does suggest is that among the sub-domains most closely associated with depression, their distribution is more structured by where people live than is the case for the sub-domains less closely associated with depression.

This pattern aligns with a rapidly maturing literature on the social determinants of mental health. Kirkbride and colleagues' recent comprehensive review [56] synthesises evidence that adverse social circumstances — economic precarity, food and housing insecurity, social exclusion, and disease-related stigma — shape mental health trajectories across the life course, often operating at the community rather than the individual level. In LMIC settings specifically, Lund and colleagues [50] documented a bi-directional cycle between poverty and common mental disorders, and Ridley and colleagues [48] mapped the mechanisms through which material deprivation translates into depression and anxiety. Food insecurity — the closest real-world analogue of our basic-needs sub-domain — has been independently associated with depression and anxiety across 149 countries in Jones's global analysis [49] with effects robust to individual-level socioeconomic adjustment. Stigma, in turn, has been documented as a prominent companion to acute disease outbreaks [53], particularly in LMIC settings where it intersects with pre-existing social fault-lines; its cross-cultural semantics are heterogeneous across Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam (*malu* in Indonesian; the Sanskritic *lajja* and the colloquial Nepali *laj*; *xấu hổ* in Vietnamese), and broader evidence on idioms of distress in South and Southeast Asian settings [54] indicates that such terms encode overlapping but non-identical configurations of shame, moral exposure, and loss of locally valued standing [47]. That our two most site-clustered worry domains — material and symbolic — are also the two most closely associated with depression is consistent with the interpretation that the depressive burden of public-health shocks in LMIC settings is patterned as strongly by community context as by individual characteristics, a reading that identifies community-level social determinants as a plausible target of response and not only as a background confounder.

## 4.5 Implications for mental health response in LMICs

If different domains of worry are associated with different dimensions of distress, and if some of those domains are heavily patterned by community context, several programmatic considerations follow. First, generalist "worry reduction" or broad psychological first aid may be insufficiently targeted. In settings where basic-needs and safety/stigma concerns carry most of the depressive signal, interventions that address symptomatology without engaging its social stressors are unlikely to reach the populations for whom the need is greatest. The accumulating evidence from cash-transfer programmes in LMICs — including Haushofer and Shapiro's randomised evaluation of unconditional transfers in rural Kenya, which reported substantial improvements in psychological well-being [55], McGuire and colleagues' meta-analysis of 45 studies covering 116,999 individuals across 22 low- and middle-income countries [57], and the case for direct material support developed by Patel and colleagues in the Lancet Commission on global mental health and sustainable development [5] — indicates that material support can produce reproducible improvements in subjective well-being and common mental disorders. The pooled effect sizes are small (for mental health, Cohen's *d* ≈ 0.07 [57]) and fall short of clinical treatment effects, but the intervention is highly scalable, does not require specialist workforces, and addresses stressors that both our data and Kirkbride and colleagues' review [56] identify as community-patterned. On this reading, material support belongs in the public-health response package during future crises, alongside rather than instead of psychological intervention.

Second, where stigma concerns are prominently associated with distress, anti-stigma communication anchored in local moral experience [47,54] — including community-level interventions that engage what locally matters most rather than relying solely on individual-level education — is likely to be more effective than generic destigmatisation messaging, particularly given the site-clustering we observed. Third, where direct psychological intervention is indicated, task-sharing models delivered by trained non-specialist providers offer scalable pathways in settings where specialist mental health workforces are thin. Chibanda and colleagues' Friendship Bench trial in Zimbabwe [52] and the broader portfolio of LMIC-tested interventions synthesised by Singla and colleagues [51] demonstrate that problem-solving therapy and related brief structured interventions delivered by lay providers can meaningfully reduce symptoms of common mental disorders; translating these protocols into acute-crisis response is a practical near-term priority. The differential associations we document do not directly identify which intervention element is most effective for which symptom dimension; they indicate that intervention design should account for differential exposure pathways rather than treating crisis-related worry as a monolithic stressor. Fourth, our finding that higher comorbidity count was independently associated with greater distress across all three DASS-21 dimensions, and with one of the largest coefficients in the model (β ≈ 0.23–0.25 per condition), echoes the broader evidence on mental–physical multimorbidity in LMIC settings and reinforces the case for integrated service delivery that does not silo mental and physical health.

Fifth, these programmatic implications do not transport uniformly to Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam, whose mental-health delivery systems differ structurally. Indonesia's *Puskesmas* primary-care network provides an existing platform suited to integrating cash-linked psychosocial support at community level; Vietnam's commune health-station infrastructure offers a decentralised route for community-based anti-stigma communication anchored in local moral experience; and Nepal's continuing shortage of specialist mental-health providers strengthens the case for task-sharing with trained lay counsellors along the lines demonstrated by Chibanda and colleagues [52]. Intervention design in each setting should be anchored in the delivery platform already present rather than treated as a pan-LMIC template, and the differential exposure patterns we document (§3.4) should inform which platform-compatible programmatic element is prioritised.

## 4.6 Strengths and limitations

Strengths of the study include a theoretically motivated decomposition of the worry inventory validated by split-sample exploratory and confirmatory factor analysis; metric and partial scalar measurement invariance testing across three country contexts; Rubin-pooled multilevel regression across 30 imputations with Benjamini–Hochberg adjustment of the pre-specified 15-test family; Rubin-pooled mediation using the Schomaker–Heumann framework [44]; and an extensive battery of pre-specified and post-hoc sensitivity analyses, all of which reproduced the primary ordering of worry × outcome associations (§3.7; Tables S12–S17). The multi-country community sample, drawn from urban, peri-urban, rural, and remote settings across Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam, offers structural heterogeneity that contrasts with the predominantly single-country designs represented in the regional pandemic meta-analyses [59,60].

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgement. First, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference; the mediation results should be read as a decomposition of observed covariation under a hypothesised direction rather than as causal estimates [43]. Second, data were collected during February–June 2021, which spans different national phases of the public-health shock; although country fixed effects partly absorb this heterogeneity, the results should not be extrapolated to other phases of a crisis, nor assumed to generalise to pre- or post-crisis states. Third, the 13-site cluster count falls below Maas–Hox thresholds [38] for reliable variance-component interpretation and cross-level inference; we accordingly do not interpret site-level variance components or individual site random-intercept estimates (Note S1), and within-country cluster counts (*k* = 3–6) further constrain country-stratified estimates to hypothesis-generating status. Fourth, the purposive site-selection design of the parent SPEAR study [21], together with non-support for full scalar measurement invariance (§3.2), limits the external-validity warrant for cross-country comparisons of distress levels. Fifth, the structural-equation specification of the same measurement–structural model yielded improper solutions in both five-factor and four-factor configurations (Note S2), indicating empirical under-identification at this level of worry-sub-domain disaggregation; the multilevel regression therefore remains the primary analytic framework, with the SEM reported as a directional convergence check only. Sixth, the safety/stigma sub-domain comprises only two items; cross-cultural semantics of stigma in Indonesia, Nepal, and Vietnam differ substantially [47,54], and a two-item index cannot disambiguate these meanings or capture the structurally distinct moral experiences that Yang and colleagues' framework identifies as central to stigma's psychological impact. Seventh, the heterogeneity of idioms of distress across South and Southeast Asian settings [54] means that DASS-21 and the worry inventory, although measurement-invariant at the metric level and partially so at the scalar level (§3.2), may each capture slightly different facets of the local suffering landscape across countries; our country-stratified estimates should be read with this caveat. Finally, all measures are self-reported and administered in a single interview session, which introduces the possibility of common-method bias beyond what our Harman's single-factor post-hoc check (Table S15) can rule out; furthermore, the DASS-21's seven-day recall window and the implicit reference period of the bespoke worry items (not anchored to a specific window in the instrument) may not align, and any such recall-window mismatch could modestly attenuate the cross-sectional associations we estimate. In addition, unmeasured site-level confounders (including local outbreak timing, urbanicity gradients within a site label, and pre-existing community mental health infrastructure) cannot be fully absorbed by site fixed effects at *k* = 13 and may contribute to the country- and site-specific variation observed in §3.6.

## 4.7 Future directions and conclusion

Three directions for future work follow directly from the findings. First, longitudinal designs are needed to test whether the pathway-specific associations we document reflect temporally ordered proliferation, as the Stress Process Model predicts, or represent a synchronous clustering of concerns and symptoms; this is the decisive test that cross-sectional data cannot deliver [43]. Second, intervention trials specifically designed to examine whether material support, anti-stigma community interventions, and non-specialist-delivered psychological therapy [51,52] produce differential effects on depression, anxiety, and stress as predicted by the selective specificity pattern would translate the descriptive finding into a prescriptive hypothesis. Third, conceptual and measurement work remains on the safety/stigma domain in particular: the two-item index used here was powered to capture a single axis of concern and should be expanded in future instrument development to reflect the multidimensional semantic space of stigma across cultural contexts [46,47,54].

Psychological distress during a public-health shock in vulnerable LMIC communities is not a uniform response to generalised threat. Decomposing crisis-related worry into its material, interpersonal, and symbolic sub-domains reveals a patterned mapping between specific types of concern and specific dimensions of distress — selective pathway specificity — that aggregate scoring obscures. The worry sub-domains most strongly patterned by community context (basic-needs worry and safety/stigma) are also those most strongly associated with depression, suggesting that the depressive burden of public-health crises in LMICs is closely patterned by structural inequalities in material security and social standing. Replicated in longitudinal and intervention research, these findings would support more differentiated, structurally-anchored mental health responses to future public-health crises in resource-constrained settings.
